Research

Working papers and publications on digital economy measurement, intangible assets, and economic statistics.

Working Papers
Measuring the Value of Free Digital Services
This study estimates and examines the value derived by households from the utilisation of free digital services — specifically videoconferencing, personal email, and online news. We employ the prices of "premium" or paid internet services as a proxy for the value of their free counterparts, and use hedonic regression to extract the value of the 'free component' of these products. Our estimates show that in 2020, the aggregate gross value derived by households from the three digital services was between £7.0 billion and £25.4 billion, equivalent to 0.57–2% of household final consumption expenditures (HFCE). The value derived is growing much faster than HFCE; in 2020, real HFCE decline would have been 0.07–0.13 percentage points slower had the value of these services been incorporated in the estimates.
📄 Discussion Paper 📋 World KLEMS Proceedings
Measuring the Value of Digital Piracy
This study measures the value derived by households from digital piracy, employing the price of paid digital services (e.g. Spotify, Netflix) as a proxy for the shadow price of their illegally acquired counterparts — a method consistent with the valuation of non-market activity for national accounting purposes. Using data from the UK Intellectual Property Office's annual Piracy Tracker, we find that on average, the gross value from the piracy of music, video, live sports, software, computer games, and ebooks was between £3.6 billion and £7.5 billion in 2021. While the value from final consumption of communication services has been consistently rising, the inflation-adjusted gross value from digital piracy of media has been falling.
📄 Discussion Paper
Measuring the Depreciation of Intangible Assets Using Search Volume Data
This study introduces a novel methodology to estimate the depreciation of intangible assets — specifically software and creative originals — using Google Search Volume (GSV) data. Depreciation is understood as obsolescence: as intangible assets become obsolete, their capacity to generate future output diminishes. GSV provides a practical gauge of product relevance, and the decline in search activity over time aligns with economic depreciation principles. Using Poisson Pseudo-Maximum-Likelihood (PPML) and negative binomial regressions, we find a depreciation rate for software originals of 13.4–19.4%, lower than the 20–25% typically employed by statistical agencies. Movie depreciation rates are comparable to US and German estimates. Applying our software rates would raise TFP growth for the Information and Communication industry notably from 1996–2008 and 2011–2016.
